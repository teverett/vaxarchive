General Notes

Cable option numbers tend to be of the format: 

                          BCxxx-yy     or     BNxxx-zz

where the "C" indicates that the suffix "yy" describes the length of the cable in feet, for example

BC21M-09...is a BC21M cable 9 feet long, and the "N" means the length "zz" is described in meters.

BN25B-30 is a BN25B cable 30 metres long.

Some cable options are prefixed with an "E0" identifier, this simply indicates the European origin of the cable, for example E0-BCC05-50 is a 50 feet long BCC05 sourced in Europe.

Other Cable Lengths

Letters may be used for cable lengths, eg,

A = 100,

B = 200,

C = 300,

D = 400,

E = 500,

H = 700,

L = 1000,

for example

-A5 = 150,

-B5 = 250,

-C0 = 300,

-H5 = 750,

-L0 = 1000, so.......

BN25B-H5 is a 750m BN25B cable, and BC22D-B5 is 250 feet long BC22D.

These letters may also be used to indicate decimals or feet and inches, thus BN19A-2E is a 2.5m BN19A, and BC26V-6D is a 6 feet 4 inches long BC26V.

Appendix A EIA Standards, Speed and Distance

Since 1985, the Electrical Industries Association (EIA), decided to replace the 'RS-' identifier with EIA', therefore RS-232-C has been replaced by EIA-232-D. These two standards are compatible with each other. The EIA-232-D/CCITT V.24/V.28 standard was originally designed to specify the connection between a local interface and a modem. It was not intended to be used for connecting to terminals over long distances. The maximum specified length is 50 feet (15m), and shielded cable must be used in order to meet the requirements of FCC and VDE Radio Frequency Interference (RFI) regulations.

Although cable lengths of greater than 50 feet can be used with reasonable success, cable capacitance, noise, and ground potential difference restrict the line speed as the distances increase.

As a consequence of this, the performance of long distance communications to a terminal using EIA-232 does not meet today's requirements for terminal wiring. DEC423 is a superset of the RS-423-A/CCITT V.10 standard. RS-423-A has a different grounding and signal return path arrangement from EIA-232-D, as well as using different line driver and receiver chips. DEC423 uses line driver and receiver chips which have better filtering and tighter level tolerances than those specified by RS-232-A. In addition, DEC423 devices include transient suppressors for electrical overstress (EOS) and electro-static discharge (ESD) protection. DEC423 devices may also be connected with unshielded cable. The features provided by DEC423 devices provide reliable data communication over increased distances, typically 1000 feet (300m).

DEC423 uses the RJ45 Modular Jack, or the Modified Modular Jack (MMJ) connector instead of the much larger D-type connector defined in RS-449. DEC423 is signal compatible with the EIA-232-D standard when used for data-leads-only interconnection, ie, interconnection between devices using the different standards is possible but the speed and distance restrictions of EIA-232-D will still apply. DEC423 should always be used be used in preference to EIA-232-D for direct terminal connection over extended distances.

EIA-232-D terminals can be connected to DEC423 devices using either an H8571-x passive adapter, or a H3105-B active adapter. The H3105-B active terminal adapter is necessary when using an EIA-232-D terminal with a DEC423 interface if the longer cable lengths obtainable with DEC423 are required.

There are two types of DEC423 cable available: the H8245/6 "Building" cable which is of twisted pair construction, and the H8240 "Office" or flat 6-core ribbon cable. BC16E cables are pre-terminated lengths of H8240 cable. The maximum length for DEC423 cabling has been given, for example, 1000 feet at 19,200 Baud, and within this, 50 feet is the usually recommended maximum length of "office" cabling, however 100 feet would probably give satisfactory results.

DEC422 also incorporates EOS/ESD protection and RS-422 compatible drivers and receivers, thus DEC422 is ideal for use in high noise environments. DEC422, which uses differential line drivers supports cable lengths of up to 4000 feet (1200m) at all supported speeds.

Appendix H - Fibre Optics Notes

The fibre optic cables supplied by Digital such as the BN24B, BN24D, or BN24E are for internal use ONLY because they do not have a weather proofed or amoured jacket. 90% of fibre cable applications require that the cable be run externally either in a duct or even directly buried in the ground. It is a specialist job to define the cable required, the possible maximum lengths and the termination for a particular site and a particular application, so an in depth site survey will be required. Customers who do not have access to the necessary skills should be directed to their local Digital office or their account manager to IBS (Intelligent Building Services) who can provide a total installed and tested solution.

In general, the Digital recommendation for a new fibre optic installation is for 62.5/125 micron, dual window, graded-index profile, multimode glass-on-glass construction (EIA Class 1A). Digital is optimising its multimode fibre products for 62.5/125 fibre.

Connectors

Types of fibre-optic connectors in current use:-

ST - Bayonet

This type of connector has a "push-twist" fastening action. It virtually replaced the older SMA-type which has a screw-on fastening. The ST provides a higher reliability and a better connection than the SMA.

ST - Push-pull

The push-pull construction provides easy insertion and high repeatability.

SC

This connector is physically smaller than the ST-type, has rectangular cross-section, and has a push-pull fixing action. There is both a simplex and a duplex version.

Digital is using SC connectors in certain products because of the smaller physical size and also industry demand. The SC connector is specified in TIA/EIA 568-A. The duplex SC socket is considerably smaller than the widely used MIC socket.

MIC

The FDDI MIC (Media Interface Connector) is a duplex connector. The MIC is keyed, and is field programmable for the appropriate type of FDDI connection.

FC/PC

Digital chose the FC/PC connector for SM (Single Mode) fibre applications. One of the key selection factors was that FC-PC is physically different to the ST and thus customers could not inadvertently plug SM into MM (Multi Mode) sockets.
